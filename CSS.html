Ebola disease

The World Health Organisation declared a public health emergency of international concern on 17 May 2026, following reports of an outbreak of Ebola, caused by the Bundibugyo virus, in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda.

Ebola disease is a severe, often fatal illness caused by viruses of the Orthoebolavirus genus in the Filoviridae family. The main species causing large outbreaks are Ebola virus (EBOV), Sudan virus (SUDV), and Bundibugyo virus (BDBV). The disease has a high case fatality rate, averaging around 50%, and can range from 25% to 90% in past outbreaks. Ebola is transmitted through direct contact with blood, body fluids, or tissues of infected humans or animals, and is not airborne. Symptoms typically appear 2–21 days after exposure and include fever, fatigue, muscle pain, headache, sore throat, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and sometimes bleeding



WHO advises no international travel or trade restrictions, but recommends strengthening preparedness, cross-border collaboration, and public awareness. Countries hosting mass gatherings, such as the 2026 World Cup, are considered at low risk for Ebola transmission, though monitoring and preventive measures are in place . Travelers returning from affected areas should monitor their health for 21 days and seek immediate medical care if symptoms develop
